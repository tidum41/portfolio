01 Overview

Finding housing near UCLA is broken

Students rely on scattered Facebook groups, Reddit threads, and word-of-mouth to find sublease listings — with no standardized pricing, dates, or verification. This created a clear opportunity for a purpose-built platform.

UCLA Sublease is a housing platform that connects students looking to sublease their apartments with others searching for short-term housing near campus. I led design from 0→1 over six months.

02 Problem

Three pain points, one broken flow

  • ✕ No standardized information. Posts vary wildly — some have prices, some don't. No consistent format for dates, amenities, or contact info.
  • ✕ No trust layer. Students can't verify listings or lessors. Scams are common and there's no accountability system in place.
  • ✕ Fragmented across platforms. Listings live in Facebook groups, ULoop, Reddit, and group chats simultaneously — impossible to browse comprehensively.

research — competitive analysis map

We interviewed 24 UCLA students who had subleased or attempted to sublease in the past year. We mapped their full journey and identified the key breakpoints where friction was highest.

04 Design decisions

Standardized listing format

The core hypothesis: if we enforce a consistent listing schema, search becomes dramatically easier for renters and lessors spend less time writing and more time getting matches.

decision 1 — listing card iterations (initial → condensed → final)

After three rounds of testing, the condensed card with a price-first hierarchy performed best. Renters scanned price in under 0.3s, vs. 1.4s for the initial layout.

05 Reflection

What I'd do differently

Ship faster, test earlier

We spent too long in wireframe fidelity before getting screens in front of real users. The listing card we thought was worst turned out to be clearest in testing.

Involve lessors from day one

Our early research skewed toward renters. The friction for lessors creating listings was discovered late and required a late-stage redesign of the creation flow.

Trust signals matter most

Post-launch data showed that the verification badge on listings drove the highest click-through rate — more than price, location, or photos.

Design for the desperate case

UCLA housing is seasonal and urgent. Designing for a calm, exploratory browse was wrong. The real use case is a stressed student who needs something in two weeks.
